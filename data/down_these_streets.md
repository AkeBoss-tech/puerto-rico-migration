Here is a breakdown of how to map Piri Thomas’s narrative themes directly to the hard data you have collected.

### **1. The "Barrio" as a construct of Displacement**
**The Narrative:** Piri describes the tenements of Spanish Harlem (El Barrio) as overcrowded, freezing in winter, and suffocating in summer. He writes about the "Mean Streets" not just as a location, but as a trap.
**The Research Reality:**
El Barrio didn't just "happen." It was created by **Urban Renewal** (often called "Negro Removal" by activists of the time).
* **The Data Point:** In the 1950s, projects like the Lincoln Square development (which became Lincoln Center) displaced over 7,000 low-income families, disproportionately Puerto Rican. These families were pushed into already crowded areas like East Harlem and the South Bronx.
* **Interactive Idea:** Overlay a 1950s "Slum Clearance" map (showing condemned blocks) with a heat map of Puerto Rican population density. You will likely see the population spiking in El Barrio exactly when the bulldozers started in the Upper West Side.


### **2. The "Racial Data Paradox" (The Core Conflict)**
**The Narrative:** The central conflict of the book is Piri’s struggle with his **Afro-Puerto Rican identity**. He fights with his brother José, who claims to be "White/Trigueño," while Piri identifies as Black. Piri faces Jim Crow racism in the South, realizing his "Puerto Rican" status doesn't protect him from anti-Blackness.
**The Research Reality:**
The data completely erased people like Piri.
* **The Data Point:** In the 1950 Census, enumerators (census takers) often determined race by observation. As a result, **92%** of Puerto Ricans in the continental U.S. were classified as "White".
* **The Disconnect:** While Piri was living a "Black" experience in the streets, the data claimed his community was almost entirely white. It wasn't until later decades that "Some Other Race" became the dominant category.
* **Interactive Idea:** A side-by-side comparison:
    * *Left Side:* "The Official Count" (A pie chart of 1950 Census Race Data: 92% White).
    * *Right Side:* "The Lived Reality" (A quote from Piri Thomas about being denied service or treated as Black).

### **3. The "Hustle" vs. The Factory (Economic Survival)**
**The Narrative:** Piri and his friends often turn to drugs, theft, and the "hustle" to survive. He describes a lack of legitimate work options that pay a living wage.
**The Research Reality:**
Piri’s generation was the victim of a massive **economic timing mismatch**.
* **The Data Point:** Puerto Ricans arrived to work in manufacturing just as those industries began to leave New York City. Between 1950 and 1969, NYC lost roughly **200,000 manufacturing jobs** (mostly in apparel and light industry).
* **The Result:** The low-skill "ladder" jobs that earlier immigrants (Italians, Irish) used to rise out of poverty were pulled away just as Puerto Ricans stepped onto the ladder.
* **Interactive Idea:** A line chart showing "Puerto Rican Migration to NYC" (Going Up) overlaid with "Manufacturing Jobs in NYC" (Going Down). The intersection is the "Crisis Point."

### **4. The "Nuyorican" Limbo (Circular Migration)**
**The Narrative:** Near the end of the book, Piri goes to Puerto Rico. He expects to feel at home ("Mi Tierra"), but he is rejected there too. He is seen as a "Yankee" and criticized for his broken Spanish. He realizes he belongs to a new group: The **Nuyorican**.
**The Research Reality:**
This defines the "Circular Migration" data.
* **The Data Point:** During the 1960s and 70s, return migration was high, but many returnees found themselves unable to reintegrate into the island economy, leading to a "revolving door" where families moved back and forth repeatedly.
* **The "Brain Drain" Myth:** Historically, it was believed only the poor left PR. However, research shows a mix, but those returning to PR often faced higher unemployment than those who stayed in the US.
